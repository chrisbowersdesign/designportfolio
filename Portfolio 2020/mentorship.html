Web application

CollegeVine, Mentorship

One-on-one mentorship to help high school students manage their time and build a resume that showcases their interests.

  • Team

    Design lead, Chris Bowers

    Design, Kara Black

  • What I did

    Project Management, Strategy, Research, Product Design, Illustration

  • Date

    Fall 2017

When we first sat down with CollegeVine’s founders, the mentorship program was in full swing with hundreds of students enrolled. The program was spread across trello, google docs, spreadsheets, and CollegeVine software, making it challenging for mentors to manage.

The heart and soul of this service is regular video calls between mentors and mentees. We set out to design a dedicated web app that would improve the service behind these successful calls.

Key objectives:

  • 1. Engage students beyond scheduled video calls.
  • 2. Unify the mentorship program.
  • 3. Give parents more transparency and assurance.

01

Research

In addition to understanding our users, we developed a research plan to target the following:

  • Uncover the value customers perceive they’re getting from Mentorship
  • Uncover opportunities through unmet needs of parents and students
  • Uncover inefficiencies in the internal workflow
  • De-risk investment into Mentorship for CollegeVine

I led in person and remote interviews with parents, students, mentors, and management. My partner led our survey efforts to gather more information from parents and students both in and out of the program.

From the existing curriculum, we identified that the year plan needed to be revised. Mentors would map out a student’s entire year by month after only a couple of meetings. This level of granularity led to generic plans that made parents question the program.

02

Exploration

Based on our research findings, we targeted pain points in the program for exploration. Some of the questions that lead our exploration were:

  • How do we organize a year plan that can be updated over time but doesn’t feel empty at the start?
  • How do ongoing tasks differ from small tasks in this system?
  • What level of granularity will put a parent’s concerns at ease without promising future college admissions?

03

Execution

Engaging students

When a student has extra time, they want to know what to work on so they can focus quickly.

High school students have busy schedules, and they need to juggle several different goals and assignments at a time. During this phase, we reviewed different groupings for the to-do list with students and mentors. They preferred to focus on the current week with some future or ongoing tasks shown in case they get ahead or need reminding.

Connecting the dots

When a week ends, mentors want to reference the year plan so they can update the to-do list.

Instead of listing goals across multiple weeks, we grouped them by category. This way mentors can add to a manageable list as they get to know their mentees.

Some goals have a predictable list of tasks to complete. Referencing project management tools, we linked the year plan to the to-do list. Now a mentor could create an action plan that can be added to the to-do list when it’s ready to be worked on.

Visibility for parents

As the program progresses, parents want updates so they can know how to help their children.

The program created monthly reports to involve parents in the mentorship process, but they were too granular and used graphs that didn’t tell a meaningful story. We prioritized update messages from the mentor and manager, and added a commenting feature for individual goals.

04

Reflections

I wish I had access to CollegeVine’s Mentor program when I was in high school. With this in mind, I was careful not to rely on my personal biases during this project. From our research and exploration, we identified additional features that were out of scope. Some of them being a calendar view for the year plan, and a card view for the to-do list. Due to the nature of this project, we were not able to see it through to implementation and usability testing.
